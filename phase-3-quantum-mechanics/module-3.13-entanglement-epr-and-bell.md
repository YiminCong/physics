## 1. Entanglement & the EPR Paradox

**Definition.** A two-particle state is **entangled** if it cannot be written as a product of single-particle states. The canonical example is the **spin singlet** (Module 3.11): (↑↓ − ↓↑)/√2. Neither particle has a definite spin alone, yet the pair is perfectly correlated. In 1935 **Einstein, Podolsky & Rosen (EPR)** argued that because measuring one particle instantly fixes the other — however far apart — quantum mechanics must be **incomplete**, with hidden variables carrying predetermined values ("local realism"). Einstein called the alternative "spooky action at a distance."

**Demonstration.** For the singlet, measuring particle A's spin along *any* axis and finding "up" forces particle B to be "down" along that axis, with certainty. The correlations hold for every measurement direction — a stronger link than any classical pre-agreement could produce.

## 2. Bell's Theorem

**Definition.** **John Bell (1964)** turned the philosophy into an experiment. Any **local hidden-variable** theory must satisfy an inequality — the **CHSH inequality**, ⟨S⟩ ≤ 2 — bounding the correlations between distant measurements. Quantum mechanics predicts a violation, reaching **2√2** (Tsirelson's bound).

**Demonstration.** Measuring the singlet's spin correlations at cleverly chosen relative angles yields S = 2√2 > 2. Experiments — Aspect (1982) through loophole-free tests (2015) — confirm the violation. **Nature is not locally realistic**: the 2022 Nobel Prize (Clauser, Aspect, Zeilinger) recognized this.

**Application.** Entanglement is not just a paradox but a **resource**: it powers **quantum computing**, **quantum teleportation**, and **quantum cryptography**, and is the foundation of quantum information theory. It connects directly to the singlet/triplet structure (Module 3.11), identical-particle correlations (Module 3.5), and — as a macroscopic coherent state — to the paired condensate of superconductivity (Phase 5).
