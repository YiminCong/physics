# Module 8.5 — The Standard Model & Beyond

> **Phase 8 — [Particle Physics & the Standard Model](./README.md)** · Format: Definition → Demonstration → Application

## 1. The Full Standard Model

**Definition.** The **Standard Model** is the gauge field theory with group SU(3)_c × SU(2)_L × U(1)_Y whose matter content consists of three generations of quarks and leptons plus the Higgs doublet. Each generation repeats the pattern (u, d; ν_e, e), (c, s; ν_μ, μ), (t, b; ν_τ, τ), with masses increasing dramatically across generations (m_e ≈ 0.511 MeV, m_μ ≈ 106 MeV, m_τ ≈ 1.78 GeV; m_u ≈ 2 MeV, m_t ≈ 173 GeV). The gauge sector provides twelve gauge bosons: one photon (U(1)_EM), three weak bosons (W±, Z⁰), eight gluons (SU(3)_c). Masses for the W, Z, and fermions come from Yukawa couplings to the Higgs. The model is described by 19 parameters: three gauge couplings, six quark masses, three lepton masses, four CKM mixing angles, the Higgs mass and vacuum expectation value, and the QCD vacuum angle θ_QCD. It is a renormalizable quantum field theory (Modules 6.5, 6.6) — all infinities can be systematically absorbed into the 19 parameters.

**Demonstration.** The quark mixing matrix **V_CKM** (Cabibbo–Kobayashi–Maskawa) relates the weak-eigenstate quarks to mass eigenstates: (d', s', b') = V_CKM (d, s, b). This 3 × 3 unitary matrix contains one irreducible complex phase δ, which produces **CP violation** — a difference between matter and antimatter interactions. CP violation is essential for explaining the matter–antimatter asymmetry of the universe (Module 8.6), though the Standard Model's CKM phase is quantitatively insufficient. **Neutrino oscillations** — the observed conversion of ν_e → ν_μ → ν_τ as neutrinos propagate — require nonzero neutrino masses, which are absent in the minimal Standard Model; they represent the first confirmed departure from it.

**Application.** The Standard Model has survived every accelerator test for fifty years, including the discovery of the W±, Z⁰, gluon, top quark, and Higgs boson, all at predicted masses and couplings. It correctly predicts the anomalous magnetic moments of the electron and muon (though a ~ 4σ discrepancy in g − 2 of the muon may be a first crack), rare decay rates, and the entire structure of the electroweak precision measurements at LEP.

## 2. Open Problems and Beyond-Standard-Model Physics

**Definition.** Despite its success, the Standard Model is known to be incomplete. The chief open problems are: (1) **Gravity is not included** — no renormalizable quantum theory of the graviton compatible with general relativity (Phase 7) is known. (2) **Dark matter** — 27% of the universe's energy density is non-luminous, non-baryonic matter (Module 8.6) with no Standard Model candidate. (3) **Matter–antimatter asymmetry** — the CKM phase alone cannot produce the observed baryon asymmetry. (4) The **hierarchy problem**: why is m_h ≈ 125 GeV ≪ M_Planck ≈ 10^{19} GeV? Quantum corrections drive m_h toward the Planck scale unless there is near-perfect cancellation among bare parameters — unnatural fine-tuning. (5) **Three generations**: why are there exactly three, and what sets the mass pattern?

**Demonstration.** **Supersymmetry (SUSY)** addresses the hierarchy problem by pairing each boson with a fermion superpartner (and vice versa): their loop corrections to m_h² cancel exactly, stabilizing the hierarchy if superpartner masses are ∼ 1 TeV. **Grand Unified Theories (GUTs)** embed SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1) into a single simple group (e.g., SU(5) or SO(10)) at energy ∼ 10^{16} GeV where the three running couplings meet (as foreshadowed in Module 8.2). GUTs predict proton decay (p → e⁺ + π⁰) with lifetime ∼ 10^{34} yr — not yet observed, ruling out minimal SU(5) but consistent with SO(10) SUSY. The **seesaw mechanism** explains the smallness of neutrino masses: adding very heavy right-handed neutrinos N_R with Majorana mass M ≫ v generates light neutrino masses m_ν ∼ y_ν² v²/M, naturally of order meV for M ∼ 10^{14} GeV.

**Application.** The LHC has searched exhaustively for SUSY partners up to ∼ 2 TeV without discovery, raising questions about the naturalness argument. Future colliders (FCC, ILC) will probe the Higgs sector more precisely and extend SUSY/GUT searches. The interplay of the three running couplings (Module 8.2), neutrino masses, and the cosmological evidence for dark matter (Module 8.6) are the most concrete experimental handles on the physics beyond the Standard Model.
