## 1. Parity and Its Violation

**Definition.** **Parity** P is the symmetry of spatial inversion (r → −r), turning a system into its mirror image. It was assumed to be an exact symmetry of nature until **Lee and Yang (1956)** pointed out that, while well-tested for the strong and electromagnetic forces, parity conservation had **never actually been checked for the weak interaction**. They proposed experiments to test it.

**Demonstration.** The **Wu experiment (1957)** cooled and polarized ⁶⁰Co nuclei in a magnetic field and watched their beta decay. The emitted electrons came out **preferentially opposite to the nuclear spin** — a mirror-asymmetric result. Parity is **not just violated but maximally violated** by the weak force. Lee and Yang received the 1957 Nobel Prize.

## 2. Chirality and the Structure of the Weak Force

**Definition.** Parity violation is built into the weak interaction's **chiral (V−A) structure**: the weak force couples *only* to **left-handed** fermions (and right-handed antifermions). In the massless limit only **left-handed neutrinos** and right-handed antineutrinos participate.

**Application.** This handedness is encoded directly in the **electroweak theory** (Module 8.4): the SU(2) gauge group acts only on left-handed doublets, which is *why* electroweak symmetry breaking and the Higgs mechanism take the form they do. Parity violation, together with CP violation (Module 8.5), singles out the weak interaction as the strangest of the four forces and is essential to the matter–antimatter story (Module 8.6).
